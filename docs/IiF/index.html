Great Fire of Miereki (1867)

A masterpiece in contemporary Japanese Architecture, Kengo Kuma’s GC Prostho Museum and Research Centre underpins a modern aesthetic by using traditional Japanese cedar and construction techniques such as chidori joinery. A fascinating design, this structure points to the return from Japanese architects to traditional materials, despite the prevalence of Western practices throughout the 20th century.

Secondary Sources

The Tradition of Wooden Architecture in Japan

The following source is a report given by the Agency of Cultural Affairs in Japan that outlines many characteristics in Japanese, such as post-and-beam construction, materials, roofing, and craft. There is a discussion on how these aspects of Japanese architecture take into account the natural resources surrounding them, as well as the events and patterns of nature, such as earthquakes, decay, and the movement of people.

Satoshi, Yamato. The Tradition of Wooden Architecture in Japan. PDF. Agency for Cultural Affairs, Japan, 2006.

Thinking Inside the Box: Tracing Japan's Traditional Design in Modern Japanese Architecture

An insightful report that analyses and compares Japanese traditional architecture with Western thought. The paper assesses a number of contemporary Japanese architects and works, and focuses on the idea that the Japanese home looks to embody nature, rather than observe it (like many homes in the West.) By structuring an argument around the prevalence of modernism and the ‘box’ throughout the world Matthew Kubota studies what traits allowed Japanese architecture and construction to stand the test of time.

Kubota, Matthew T. Thinking Inside the Box: Tracing Japan's Traditional Design in Modern Japanese Architecture. PDF. University of Hawaii, Manoa, 2016.
